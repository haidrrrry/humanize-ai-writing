# Your smartwatch is not a doctor

I wore a fitness tracker for two years and it made me worse at paying attention to my body, not better. Every morning I'd check a "recovery score" before I checked how I actually felt. Some days I felt fine and the watch said I was wrecked, so I took it easy. Some days I felt terrible and the watch gave me a green light, so I pushed. I was outsourcing a question only I could answer.

Health tech is everywhere now, and most of it is sold on a promise it can't keep: that more data about your body equals more control over it. Sometimes that's true. Often it isn't. The gap between those two is worth understanding before you strap another sensor to your wrist.

## What the sensors actually measure

A wearable doesn't measure sleep. It measures movement and heart rate, then guesses at sleep from those signals. The guess is decent for "asleep vs awake" and shaky for the stages (light, deep, REM) that the apps render in confident colored bars. Studies comparing consumer trackers to clinical sleep labs keep finding the same thing: they overestimate total sleep and miss the fine structure. The bars look precise. The precision is mostly design.

Heart rate is more reliable, which is why the genuinely useful features cluster there. Apple Watch's irregular-rhythm notification has caught atrial fibrillation in people who had no symptoms. That's real, and it's saved lives. But notice what makes it work: it watches for one specific, dangerous, detectable pattern and tells you to see a doctor. It doesn't try to optimize you. It flags an emergency and steps back.

## The optimization trap

Most health tech isn't aimed at emergencies. It's aimed at the daily nudge: close your rings, hit your steps, raise your score. The theory is that constant feedback builds better habits.

For some people it does. For others it quietly turns health into a game you can lose, and a number you didn't hit becomes a reason to feel bad about a body that was doing fine. There's a name for the extreme version, orthosomnia, where people get so anxious about their sleep scores that the anxiety wrecks their sleep. The tool created the problem it claimed to solve.

The deeper issue is that a metric is not the thing it measures. Steps are a proxy for movement, movement is a proxy for health, and somewhere in that chain the proxy becomes the goal. You start walking laps of your kitchen at 11pm to hit 10,000, a number that came from a 1960s Japanese pedometer marketing campaign, not from medicine.

## Where the tech earns its place

None of this means throw the watch in a drawer. It means use it for what it's good at and ignore the rest.

Continuous glucose monitors have changed daily life for people with diabetes in a way that's hard to overstate: fewer finger pricks, fewer dangerous lows, real-time warning before a crash. Telehealth let my grandmother see her cardiologist without a two-hour drive she couldn't make. A blood-pressure cuff that syncs to an app helps a doctor see trends instead of one nervous reading in a clinic. These work because they answer a specific question for a person who needs that answer.

The pattern holds across all of it. Health tech is worth having when it catches something you can't feel, connects you to care you couldn't reach, or manages a condition you actually have. It's worth questioning when its main job is to score a healthy body and sell you the anxiety of keeping the score up.

## A simple test

Before you trust a health gadget, ask what question it answers and whether you needed that question answered. A diabetic checking glucose needs the number. A healthy person checking their "readiness" every morning mostly needs to look out the window and notice how they feel.

The body you're tracking already sends signals. Hunger, soreness, energy, mood. The best thing technology can do for your health is point you toward those signals when they're easy to miss, then get out of the way.
